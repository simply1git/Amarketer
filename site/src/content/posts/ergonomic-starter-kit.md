The ergonomics industry wants to sell you a $400 chair. But most desk discomfort comes from three fixable geometry problems — screen too low, feet unsupported, wrists on a hard edge — and each has a solution under $30. Buy these first; *then* decide if you still need the chair.

## The kit, in the order that matters

### 1. Screen height — laptop stand or monitor riser (~$20–35)
If you look down at your screen all day, your neck pays for it and no cushion anywhere will fix that. Eye level with the top third of the screen is the rule. This purchase comes first; everything else is refinement.

### 2. Footrest (~$15–30)
If your feet dangle or perch on the chair base, your lower back is doing extra work every hour. A footrest re-levels your whole sitting posture. **Look for:** adjustable angle, non-slip surface. A firm box works as a $0 trial — if it helps, buy the real one.

### 3. Chair seat cushion (~$20–30)
Turns the kitchen chair or the tired office chair you already own into something workable for full days. **Look for:** high-density foam (memory foam flattens), washable cover. This is the honest alternative to the $400 chair for a business that's still at $0 revenue.

### 4. Wrist rest or desk pad (~$12–25)
Hard desk edge under your wrists, hours a day, does slow damage. A padded desk pad solves it and improves the desk generally — we covered it in the [under-$50 upgrades list](/posts/desk-upgrades-under-50).

## The honest part

No product fixes sitting badly for ten hours. The kit works alongside two free habits: stand up briefly every hour, and keep your most-used items close enough that you don't twist for them. Gear plus habits, under $100 total — *then* judge whether the expensive chair still feels necessary.
